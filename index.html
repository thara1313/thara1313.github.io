Abstract

This project investigates how UC San Diego can improve the reliability of its bus fleet and make better use of electric shuttles while moving toward long-term zero-emission goals. We compare UCSD’s current shuttle system with University of California policies and California zero-emission bus regulations, and we benchmark UCSD against another UC campus, UC Irvine.

Our early findings show that UCSD’s electric bus adoption is still low (6 electric buses out of 30 total), which puts the campus far behind UC system expectations and state-level sustainability targets. This website summarizes our methods, key data, and main insights.

UC & State Policy Analysis

UC Policy states that campuses should move toward 100% zero-emission fleets by 2045. Under the UCOP Sustainable Practices Policy, all new fleet vehicle purchases are expected to be zero-emission by 2027.

UCSD currently operates 6 electric buses out of 30 total, which is about 20% electrification. Compared to the UC system’s expectation of 100% ZEV for new fleet purchases, UCSD is roughly 80 percentage points behind.

This gap suggests that UCSD must significantly accelerate its transition planning, expand charging infrastructure, and make better use of the electric shuttles that already exist.

Comparing UCSD with UCI

UCI (Anteater Express)

UCI runs a smaller, more compact shuttle network that mainly circles the campus and nearby housing. This simpler system made it easier for UCI to transition to a fully electric bus fleet.

UCSD (Triton Transit)

UCSD operates a larger and more complex network with long routes that connect main campus, medical centers, and surrounding areas. This scale increases operational challenges for electrification and reliability.

The main difference between the two systems is scale and route complexity. UCI’s compact system makes electrification easier, while UCSD’s large and branching routes require more vehicles, more charging capacity, and more carefully planned scheduling.

Data Highlights

  • UCSD fleet: 24 CNG shuttles and 6 electric shuttles (20% electric).
  • Ridership and total vehicles increased between 2022/2023 and 2024/2025, indicating higher demand and more pressure on the system.
  • Rising demand plus slow electrification creates a risk of unreliable service and missed sustainability targets.

Project Team & Goals

Our team is part of a public health course at UC San Diego. We combine interests in sustainability, transportation planning, data analysis, and visual design.

  • Clarify how far UCSD is from current UC and California sustainability goals.
  • Identify practical steps to improve reliability and expand electric shuttle use.
  • Share our findings with students, staff, and decision-makers in an accessible way.
